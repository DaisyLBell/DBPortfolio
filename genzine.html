Color Palette

When I became the Creative Director of GEN-ZiNE, I brought collaboration to the creative culture to ensure brand cohesion and inspire collective creativity.

Before designing each zine, I had designers on the zine team pitch one or more color palettes that they think best represents the zine’s theme. Volume 2 Issue 2’s theme is spectrum —identity spectrum, political spectrum, gender spectrum, etc— and Volume 2 Issue 3's is renaissance. While this part of development is essential to visual cohesion in print design, digital design for our brand did not require such steadfast commitment to one color palette. The social media designers were explore their use of color. The color palettes for the zines can be seen above. They were selected by the zine team to represent each respective issue.

Collaborative moodboards

Following the color palette selection, each design team collaborated on their team's collective moodboard. By engaging in this exercise, the designers were able to form working relationships with each other and align their unique vision for each visual deliverable —of course, while adapting to brand guidelines. I find that this step in design development, and also in creative projects, is critical to ensure visual cohesion.

Rough drafts

While having rounds of drafts is the bane of about all visual designers' existence, I find that they are some of the most important deliverables in any production process. Rough drafts have the ability to foster collaboration and align designers with the greater impact of their visual products. As an avid fan of what I call "ideation drafts" —really just a term to describe each aspect of a design having the capacity to have its own edits, I brought my fan of drafts to my teams. This stage of our production processes —both in designing for print and for digital markerting— ensured our visuals represented the brand, its content writing, and the delivery of high-quality final deliverables.
